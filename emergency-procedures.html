The RBMK Emergency Operating Procedures Manual provides critical guidance for reactor operators during abnormal and emergency conditions. These procedures are designed to prevent reactor damage, ensure personnel safety, and minimize radiological releases during off-normal events. All operators must be thoroughly trained in these procedures and demonstrate proficiency through regular simulator exercises.

CRITICAL WARNING: AZ-5 (АЗ-5) is an abbreviation for "emergency protection of the 5th category". Due to design characteristics, scramming an RBMK reactor could raise reactivity to dangerous levels before lowering it. Post-1986 modifications have addressed this issue, but operators must remain aware of reactor dynamics during shutdown procedures.

1.2 Immediate Action Criteria

The following conditions require IMMEDIATE reactor shutdown via AZ-5:

  • Reactivity Control: ORM below 15 rods
  • Power Excursion: Reactor period <10 seconds
  • Core Cooling: Loss of >50% cooling flow
  • Pressure Boundary: Multiple fuel channel ruptures
  • Instrumentation: Complete loss of nuclear instrumentation
  • Seismic Event: Ground acceleration >0.1g
  • Operator Judgment: Any condition threatening core integrity

2. Reactor SCRAM Procedures (AZ-5)

2.1 AZ-5 Emergency Shutdown System

AZ-5 System Description
The scram was started when the AZ-5 button of the reactor emergency protection system was pressed: this engaged the drive mechanism on all control rods to fully insert them, including the manual control rods that had been withdrawn earlier. Post-1986 modifications: AZ-5 sequence reduced from 18 to 12 seconds.

2.2 AZ-5 Initiation Procedure

IMMEDIATE ACTION - REACTOR SCRAM
  1. ANNOUNCE: "REACTOR SCRAM" over plant intercom
  2. PRESS: AZ-5 button (red mushroom button on main panel)
    • Verify AZ-5 light illuminates
    • Confirm rod motion on SKALA display
    • Control rods move at 0.4 metres per second
    • Monitor insertion time: should complete in 12 seconds
  3. VERIFY: Automatic actions
    • All control rods inserting
    • Turbine trip occurs
    • Generator breaker opens
    • ECCS standby signal
  4. MONITOR: Reactor parameters
    • Power decreasing on all ranges
    • Period meter shows negative
    • Pressure stable or decreasing
    • Core flow maintained >30%

2.3 BAZ Fast Shutdown System

The BAZ system (rapid reactor emergency protection) inserts 24 uniformly distributed rods into the reactor core via a modified drive mechanism within 1.8 to 2.5 seconds.

BAZ Activation Criteria:

  • Power excursion with period <3 seconds
  • AZ-5 failure to initiate
  • Multiple control rod drive failures
  • Severe pressure transient

2.4 Post-SCRAM Actions

✓ POST-SCRAM VERIFICATION CHECKLIST

All rods fully inserted (check SKALA display)
Reactor power <1% and decreasing
~7% decay heat generation verified
Primary circulation maintained (minimum 2 MCPs running)
Steam dump to condenser or atmosphere
Electrical loads transferred to off-site power
Emergency diesels running and loaded
Radiation monitors normal
Personnel accountability complete
Regulatory notifications made

3. Loss of Coolant Accident (LOCA) Response

3.1 LOCA Recognition

A loss-of-coolant accident (LOCA) is a mode of failure for a nuclear reactor; if not managed effectively, the results of a LOCA could result in reactor core damage. Each nuclear plant's emergency core cooling system (ECCS) exists specifically to deal with a LOCA.

LOCA Indicators:

  • Rapid decrease in primary system pressure
  • Drum separator level decreasing uncontrollably
  • Increased humidity in reactor spaces
  • High radiation alarms in reactor building
  • Acoustic monitors indicate pipe break
  • Mismatch between MCP flow and core flow

3.2 Large Break LOCA Response

CRITICAL: For RBMK reactors, the consequences of rupture of more than 11-16 fuel channels at full pressure would be close to the consequences of Chernobyl accident.

3.3 Control Rod Cooling Circuit Strategy

The channels with control rods are cooled with water supplied by the system totally independent from the reactor cooling system. The heat from fuel channels, where heat is generated, through graphite columns is transferred in radial direction to cooled channels with control rods. Therefore, the heat removal from RBMK-1500 reactor core using control rods cooling circuit can be used as non-regular mean for reactor cool-down.

4. Power Excursion Response

4.1 Positive Reactivity Insertion

WARNING - Positive Void Coefficient: The RBMK reactor possesses a "positive void coefficient". If power increases or flow decreases, there is increased steam production in the fuel channels, so neutrons that would have been absorbed by water will produce increased fission. At power <20% maximum, the positive void coefficient effect is dominant and the reactor becomes unstable.

4.3 Emergency Power Reduction

  1. If AZ-5 is NOT warranted (Period >30 seconds):
    • Insert control rods in symmetric pattern
    • Reduce recirculation flow by 25%
    • Increase feedwater flow to reduce voids
    • Open turbine bypass valves
  2. Monitor for stability:
    • Power oscillations <±5%
    • Xenon distribution symmetric
    • No local hot spots
  3. If instability persists:
    • Initiate controlled shutdown
    • Do NOT attempt rapid power changes
    • Monitor for xenon transients

5. Station Blackout Response

5.1 Loss of All AC Power

RBMK reactors use water as a coolant, circulated by electrically driven pumps. Reactor No. 4 had 1,661 individual fuel channels, requiring over 45 million litres of coolant per hour. In case of total power loss, backup diesel generators took 60-75 seconds to reach full load.

5.2 Station Blackout Immediate Actions

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS - STATION BLACKOUT
  1. VERIFY reactor scram (AZ-5 on loss of power)
  2. CONFIRM diesel generator auto-start
    • All 3 diesels should start within 15 seconds
    • Full load capability in 60-75 seconds
    • If diesels fail, initiate manual start
  3. MAINTAIN core cooling during transition
    • MCP inertial flow via counterweights
    • Natural circulation if pumps coast down
    • Open atmospheric dump valves
  4. LOAD emergency buses sequentially
    • Priority 1: One MCP per loop (5.5 MW each)
    • Priority 2: ECCS pumps
    • Priority 3: Instrumentation and control
    • Priority 4: Emergency lighting and ventilation

6. Xenon Poisoning Management

6.1 Understanding Xenon-135 Effects

The iodine pit is a temporary disabling of a nuclear reactor due to buildup of 135Xe, mainly produced by beta decay of 135I. 135I is a weak neutron absorber, while 135Xe is the strongest known neutron absorber. When 135Xe builds up in the fuel rods, it significantly lowers their reactivity.

6.2 Xenon Transient Recognition

Condition Xenon Status Reactivity Effect Operator Response
Power Reduction 135Xe building up Negative reactivity increasing Anticipate need for rod withdrawal
Post-Shutdown Peak in 1-2 days Reactor cannot restart Wait for decay (~3 days)
Power Increase 135Xe burning out Positive reactivity adding Insert rods to compensate
Flux Oscillation Period ~15 hours Local power ×3 variation Adjust rod pattern for symmetry

6.3 Prevention of Xenon-Related Instabilities

CHERNOBYL LESSON: The Chernobyl disaster occurred after recovering Reactor 4 from a nonuniformly poisoned state. Power plummeted due to 135Xe accumulation at low power. Operators withdrew most control rods attempting to bring power back up. These actions put the reactor in a state exposed to a feedback loop of neutron power and steam production.

Critical Operating Restrictions:

  • NEVER operate below 700 MW thermal for extended periods
  • NEVER violate minimum ORM of 15 rods
  • NEVER attempt rapid power recovery after xenon poisoning
  • ALWAYS maintain symmetric flux distribution
  • ALWAYS consider xenon effects in power maneuvers

7. Graphite Fire Response

7.1 Graphite Stack Protection

The graphite stack is kept in a helium-nitrogen mixture, providing an inert atmosphere for the graphite, protecting it from potential fires. Maximum allowed temperature of the graphite is up to 730°C.

7.2 Graphite Fire Prevention and Response

WARNING: Direct water application to hot graphite can cause steam explosions and hydrogen generation. Use inert gas injection as primary suppression method.
Temperature Range Condition Required Action Suppression Method
450-650°C Normal operation Monitor gas composition Maintain He/N₂ atmosphere
650-700°C Alarm condition Reduce power, increase cooling Increase N₂ injection rate
700-730°C Maximum limit Immediate power reduction Maximum inert gas flow
>730°C Fire risk SCRAM + emergency cooling CO₂ injection + N₂ flooding
>900°C Active fire Evacuate + seal building Total inert gas flooding

7.3 Fire Suppression Procedure

  1. Detection and Confirmation
    • High temperature alarms on multiple channels
    • CO/CO₂ detection in gas system
    • Visual confirmation (if accessible)
  2. Immediate Actions
    • SCRAM reactor (AZ-5)
    • Maximize inert gas injection
    • Isolate affected zones
    • Evacuate unnecessary personnel
  3. Suppression Strategy
    • Primary: Nitrogen/CO₂ injection at >10,000 m³/hr
    • Secondary: Sand/boron mixture if accessible
    • Never use water directly on graphite
    • Cool surrounding structures with water spray

9. Turbine Trip Response

9.1 Turbine Trip Without Reactor Trip

Due to online refueling capability, RBMK reactors can continue operation with one turbine offline:

  1. Automatic Actions (0-10 seconds)
    • Turbine stop valves close
    • Generator breaker opens
    • Bypass valves open to condenser
    • Power reduction to 50% (if single turbine)
  2. Operator Actions
    • Verify bypass valve operation
    • Reduce reactor power to match steam demand
    • Monitor drum separator levels
    • Adjust feedwater flow
  3. Stabilization (10-30 minutes)
    • Establish steady-state at reduced power
    • Balance steam production and removal
    • Monitor for xenon transients
    • Prepare for turbine restart or continued operation

B. Post-Accident Modifications Summary

Following the 1986 Chernobyl accident, significant modifications were made to all RBMK reactors:

  • Control Rod Redesign: The largest updates fixed the RBMK control rod design. The control rods have 4.5-metre graphite displacers, which prevent coolant water from entering the space vacated as rods are withdrawn. Displacers being shorter than core height left 1.25-metre columns of water when fully extracted
  • Faster SCRAM: AZ-5 sequence reduced from 18 to 12 seconds
  • BAZ System: Addition of BAZ system with 24 uniformly distributed rods inserting within 1.8 to 2.5 seconds
  • Operating Restrictions: Minimum power increased to 700 MW thermal
  • ORM Requirements: Strict enforcement of 15 rod minimum
  • Void Coefficient: Reduced through fuel enrichment changes
